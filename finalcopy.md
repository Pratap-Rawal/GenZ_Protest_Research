## Introduction

The 2025 Gen Z protests in Nepal represent a significant moment of youth-led political mobilization. Triggered by concerns over corruption and government regulation of social media, the protests quickly escalated into large-scale demonstrations across the country.

This study examines how media coverage of these protests evolved over time, particularly in terms of framing, tone, and attribution of violence. While media reports may appear neutral, the selection and emphasis of certain aspects of events can shape public perception of responsibility and legitimacy.

The study focuses on news articles from The Kathmandu Post and seeks to understand how media narratives changed as the protests progressed from initial clashes to fatalities and subsequent investigations.

### Expected Findings

It is expected that media framing will shift as the severity of events increases. Initial coverage may emphasize law-and-order concerns, while later coverage may focus on state repression and accountability, particularly following incidents involving casualties.

## Methodology

This study employs qualitative content analysis of news articles.

### Data Source
Articles were collected from The Kathmandu Post, focusing on coverage of the Gen Z protests during September 2025.

### Sampling
A purposive sample of six news articles was selected to represent different stages of the protest, including initial clashes, escalation, fatalities, and subsequent developments.

### Unit of Analysis
Each news article constitutes a unit of analysis.

### Coding Framework
Each article was coded based on:
- Tone (Neutral, Positive, Negative)
- Frame (Law and Order, State Repression, Democratic Rights)
- Focus (Protestors, Government, Both)
- Violence Attribution (Protestor, Police, Both)

### Analysis
The coded data were analyzed using descriptive statistics and cross-tabulation to identify patterns between framing and attribution of violence.

## Findings

### Frame Distribution

The analysis shows that the majority of articles are framed in terms of state repression (4 out of 6), while the remaining articles use a law-and-order frame (2 out of 6). This indicates a shift in media framing as events escalated.

### Frame and Attribution of Violence

A clear relationship exists between media framing and attribution of violence. Articles framed within a law-and-order perspective either emphasize protestor violence or present violence as occurring on both sides. In contrast, all articles framed in terms of state repression attribute violence exclusively to police actions.

This suggests that framing plays a critical role in shaping perceptions of responsibility. Law-and-order framing diffuses responsibility, while state repression framing concentrates it on the state.

### Tone of Coverage

All articles exhibit a neutral tone, indicating that framing differences are not driven by overt bias but by selective emphasis on specific aspects of events.

### Evolution of Coverage

Media coverage evolves over time from reporting protestor actions and clashes to emphasizing casualties, state violence, and eventually accountability through investigative reporting. Once the framing shifts toward state repression following fatalities, it persists in subsequent coverage.

## Discussion

The findings demonstrate that media framing is dynamic and responsive to the progression of events. Initial coverage reflects concerns about disorder and control, but as the severity of the situation increases, particularly with the occurrence of fatalities, the narrative shifts toward state accountability.

Importantly, the study shows that even in the absence of overt bias in tone, framing can significantly influence how responsibility is perceived. This highlights the subtle but powerful role of media in shaping public understanding of political events.

The persistence of the state repression frame after a critical turning point suggests that certain events, such as deaths, can fundamentally alter the trajectory of media narratives.

## Limitations

This study is based on a small sample of articles from a single news source, which limits the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, the coding process involves subjective interpretation, which may introduce bias.

Future research could expand the dataset and include multiple media outlets for comparative analysis.

## Conclusion

This study finds that media framing of the Gen Z protests in Nepal evolves significantly as events unfold. While early coverage emphasizes law and order, later reporting shifts toward state repression and accountability.

The strong relationship between framing and attribution of violence highlights the role of media in shaping perceptions of responsibility. Even in neutral reporting, the emphasis placed on specific aspects of events can influence how audiences interpret complex political situations.

These findings underscore the importance of critically engaging with media narratives, particularly during periods of social and political unrest.
